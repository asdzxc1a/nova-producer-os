### 2. Burn Rate Tracking

Once the budget is established and production begins, meticulous tracking of the **burn rate** is essential.

**Definition:** Burn rate is the speed at which a project spends its allocated funds. It's typically measured in dollars per day, week, or month.

**Tracking Mechanism:**

*   **Detailed Cost Reports:** Weekly (or more frequent, depending on intensity) reports detailing all expenditures categorized by budget line item.
*   **Actual vs. Budgeted Comparison:** Direct comparison of actual spending against the budgeted amounts for each category.
*   **Forecasting:** Projecting future spending based on the current burn rate and anticipated upcoming costs.
*   **System Implementation:** Utilizing robust accounting software or project management tools with financial tracking capabilities (e.g., Movie Magic Budgeting/Scheduling, dedicated production accounting software).

**Key Metrics to Monitor:**

*   **Daily/Weekly Burn Rate:** Total spend over a defined period.
*   **Departmental Burn Rate:** Spending by individual departments (e.g., Art Department burn rate, Camera Department burn rate). This helps pinpoint where overspending is originating.
*   **Cumulative Burn Rate:** Total spend from the project's commencement to date.
*   **Burn Rate vs. Schedule Progress:** Ensuring that spending is aligned with the progress of the production.

### 4. Flagging Cost Overruns and Recommending Contingency Placement

Proactive identification and management of cost overruns are critical for project success.

**Methods for Flagging Overruns:**

*   **Real-time Monitoring:** Continuously compare actual spend against the budget.
*   **Trend Analysis:** Identify spending trends that deviate from projections.
*   **Departmental Reporting:** Encourage department heads to report potential overages *before* they become significant.
*   **Regular Financial Review Meetings:** Scheduled meetings to discuss budget status, identify risks, and make decisions.
*   **Variance Reports:** Formal reports highlighting significant differences between budgeted and actual costs.

**Recommending Contingency Placement:**

Contingency is not a slush fund; it's a strategic allocation to cover unforeseen, legitimate costs.

*   **Initial Contingency Allocation:** A standard percentage of the total budget (e.g., 10-15%) is typically allocated for general contingency. This percentage can be adjusted based on the project's inherent risk profile. For "jjj," the complexity and potential security implications of the "protokol-politiki-i-bezopasnosti.md" document might warrant a higher initial contingency.
*   **Specific Contingency Allocation:** For high-risk items or areas identified during scenario modeling, a portion of the contingency can be earmarked. For example, if the security protocols are particularly novel or complex, a portion of the contingency might be reserved specifically for unforeseen security-related expenses.
*   **"On-Call" Contingency:** A small portion of the contingency can be held in reserve for truly emergent, unanticipatable issues.

**Process for Utilizing Contingency:**

1.  **Justification Required:** Any request to draw from contingency must be thoroughly justified with detailed documentation explaining the reason for the overrun and the proposed expenditure.
2.  **Approval Process:** A clear approval process must be in place, likely involving the Line Producer, Producer, and potentially the Executive Producer or financiers.
3.  **Impact Assessment:** Before drawing from contingency, we must assess the impact on the overall budget and remaining contingency.
4.  **Re-forecasting:** After a contingency draw, the budget and burn rate forecast must be updated.
